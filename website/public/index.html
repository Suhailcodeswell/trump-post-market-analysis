Trump & Markets

An Event Study · 73,380 Posts · 2009–2025

Does what Trump says affect financial markets?

Traders set alerts for his posts. Newsrooms refresh his feed. For almost twenty years, Donald Trump has been able to type a sentence and watch markets twitch.

Some of those sentences were about Bitcoin. Some threatened Iran. A few announced tariffs that knocked billions off the S&P 500 before lunch. Whether markets actually obey him is a different question, and it deserves a better answer than anecdotes and screenshots.

So I lined up 73,380 of his posts against four markets: Bitcoin, crude oil, the S&P 500, and the Nasdaq. For every post that touched one of them, I measured what the price did one day, one week, and one month later. Sometimes the pattern is striking. Sometimes it collapses the moment you look closely. Both kinds of results are on this site, because both are true.

Research, code, and design by Suhail Ahmed LinkedIn Email GitHub

Everything here is association, not causation. Markets move for a thousand reasons and a post is rarely the biggest one. Nothing on this site is financial advice.

Market 01

Bitcoin

Bitcoin never met a quiet news cycle. The largest cryptocurrency trades around the clock, in every timezone, and it reacts to politics faster than any stock exchange can open. Trump's stance on it has flipped completely. In 2019 he said it was based on thin air. By 2024 he was promising a strategic crypto reserve and courting the Bitcoin conference vote.

The dataset holds 28 posting events where Trump talked about Bitcoin or crypto. The honest summary: he is a small factor. Halving cycles, ETF flows, and macro conditions set the direction. But a handful of individual posts sat right next to violent moves, and those deserve a close look.

Start with the whole picture. Sixteen years of Bitcoin, from pocket change to six figures. Every event on this tab happened somewhere on this curve, which is why no single post can be judged without it.

October 31, 2024. Trump wished "our great Bitcoiners" a happy anniversary of the Satoshi white paper and promised to end the war on crypto. One month later, a $100k position was worth $137k. He posted into a rally that was already running, so take the credit assignment with a grain of salt. Still, +37% is +37%.

July 12, 2019. "I am not a fan of Bitcoin..." The price gave up almost 11% in a week. People still point at this tweet as proof that he moves crypto. Maybe. That summer was also full of regulatory bad news that had nothing to do with him.

March 2, 2025. He announced a U.S. Crypto Reserve, which is about as bullish as presidential news gets. Bitcoin fell 14.5% over the following week anyway. The market had priced the policy long before the post landed. Good news on the timeline is not the same thing as good news to a trader.

Same 28 events, split by whether he held office at the time. The bars say the presidency is not a multiplier. What he says, and into what kind of market he says it, matters more than the title under the handle.

Source: Power BI report built on the event study. 73,380 cleaned posts, 2009 to 2025.

Market 02

Oil

Oil is the market where humility is mandatory. Wars, OPEC quotas, pipeline outages, a cold winter in Europe. Any one of these moves crude harder than a social media post ever will, and several of them are usually happening at once.

Trump appears 705 times in the Iran-and-energy slice of the dataset, and the average week after those posts is slightly negative. As a single factor he barely registers here. The June 2025 Iran standoff is the exception that makes this tab worth reading.

Two years of crude, swinging between $55 and $90 a barrel. The early-2025 spikes track Middle East tension. Posts decorate this chart. They do not drive it.

June 2025. One bar per post, each showing the return one week later. The June 12 diplomacy post sits next to a +10.1% move, the biggest of the cluster. That same month had airstrikes, shipping threats, and supply scares. The coloring marks size, nothing else.

Read post-event returns here as timing snapshots, never as trade signals.

Market 03

S&P 500

The S&P 500 is the scoreboard for American business. Roughly 500 companies, most of the country's retirement money, and the number presidents point at when they want credit for the economy. Trump posts about it constantly. Tariffs, China, the Fed, his own stock-market scorekeeping: 1,772 posting days in the study touch this index.

The week after he posts, the index averages +0.3%. The month after, +1.5%. That sounds like a signal until you remember the index spent most of 2009 to 2025 going up no matter who was typing.

Daily closes through 2024 and 2025, from about 5,000 points to nearly 6,900. The violent April 2025 plunge and rebound is the scar tissue of tariff season.

April 8, 2025. He shared an article about auto tariffs steering jobs back to the U.S., and the index rallied 8.3% over the next week. Did the post cause it, or did it just land at the bottom of a selloff that was due for a relief rally? The chart shows price on both sides of the date. Decide for yourself.

Market 04

Nasdaq

The Nasdaq is where the tech giants live, which makes it the natural target for Trump's posts about Big Tech, censorship, and Silicon Valley money. When he goes after Google or accuses platforms of rigging elections, this is the index that should flinch.

74 tagged events. A 79% one-month win rate, the best of any market in the study, averaging +3.0%. Before anyone builds a strategy on that number: tech beat nearly everything for fifteen straight years. He was posting into the strongest tailwind in modern markets.

The index over time, with the headline numbers from the event study alongside it: average returns, win rates, and event counts.

November 2, 2020. The night before the election, he accused Big Tech of buying Biden. The Nasdaq added 6.9% over the next week. It was also the single noisiest trading week of that year, with an entire presidential election inside it. Read this one as a timestamp, not a trigger.

Software

When to invest?

This is the software half of the project. The same Python event-study engine that produced every chart on this site sits behind this calculator. Pick one of the documented posts, set an amount, and see what buying on the day of the post would have done to that money a week or a month later.

It answers exactly one question: what happened. It cannot tell you what will happen next time, and it cannot prove the post caused the move.

Documentation

About this project

I'm Suhail Ahmed, a data analyst. I built this project end to end: the Python pipeline, the SQL model, the Power BI report, and the site you are reading. It started as a simple question over a news cycle full of market-moving posts, and turned into an event study covering sixteen years of data.

The whole thing is open. Every script, every CSV, and the Power BI file are in the GitHub repository, so anyone can rerun the numbers and argue with the conclusions.

The data

  • Posts: 90,343 raw records cleaned down to 73,380 (2009 to 2025). Deleted posts, pure reposts, and empty text were removed so the analysis covers only what Trump actually wrote.
  • Prices: daily data from Yahoo Finance for the S&P 500, Nasdaq, Dow, VIX, Bitcoin, WTI crude oil, and gold.
  • Events: keyword tagging by topic (Bitcoin, oil and Iran, tariffs, Big Tech, and more), then a posting-day event study with forward returns at one day, one week, and one month.
  • Stories: 12 hand-picked events with the original post text. These feed the calculator and the narrative tabs.

Technology stack

Python

Data cleaning, VADER sentiment scoring, yfinance market pulls, the event study itself, and a DuckDB star schema

Power BI

The interactive report: price context, event timelines, in-office comparisons, conditional formatting on returns

SQL

Star schema with Fact_Posts, Fact_Market, and Dim_Date, queried with window functions and correlations

Cursor + Claude

AI-assisted development for the pipeline, DAX debugging, and this website

This site

Hand-written HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No framework. Deployed on Vercel, with charts exported from the Power BI report.

GitHub

The full repository: scripts, the data catalog, Power BI files, and this site's source

Methodology notes

  • Sentiment was scored before any market data was joined, to avoid look-ahead bias.
  • Aggregate daily sentiment shows almost no correlation with returns. The signal, where it exists, lives in specific events.
  • Every average includes every tagged event. No cherry-picked winners.
  • External factors never sleep. Wars, the Fed, and earnings were all moving these markets at the same time.
