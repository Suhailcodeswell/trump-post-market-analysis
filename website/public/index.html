Trump & Markets

Portfolio Project · Event Study

Does what Trump says affect financial markets?

For nearly two decades, Donald Trump has been one of the most watched voices in global finance. Not as a trader, but as a source of sudden, market-moving commentary.

From late-night posts about Bitcoin to warnings aimed at Iran and surprise tariff announcements, his words often land in real time while markets are open. Traders notice. Journalists notice. The question worth asking is sharper than that: is there a repeatable pattern, and can it be measured?

This project studies Trump's posting patterns against four markets: Bitcoin, crude oil, the S&P 500, and the Nasdaq. It asks whether his posts line up with price moves, how large those moves are, over what horizon they play out, and whether the timing could ever be acted on.

This is an association study, not proof of causation. Trump is rarely the only force in play, especially for oil and broad equities. Built for research and portfolio demonstration. Not financial advice.

Market 01

Bitcoin

Bitcoin is the largest cryptocurrency on earth: volatile, globally traded, and increasingly entangled with political rhetoric around digital assets. Trump's own position has swung wildly, from calling it "based on thin air" in 2019 to pledging a U.S. crypto reserve in 2025.

We identified 28 Bitcoin-related posting events in the dataset. On average, Bitcoin does not reliably follow Trump's tone. Macro cycles, ETF flows, and halving events dominate the price. Trump is a small factor in the long run, yet individual posts can coincide with sharp short-term moves, and those moments are worth studying one by one.

Context first. Bitcoin's full price history from 2009 through 2025. The asset spent years near zero before its explosive run, so every event below has to be read against this backdrop, not in isolation.

The positive case. Oct 31, 2024. Trump posted about Bitcoin's anniversary and promised to end the "war on crypto." One month later, a $100k hypothetical entry was up +37.4%, one of the strongest forward returns in the entire dataset. Worth remembering: the 2024 bull market was already in motion before he hit publish.

The negative case. Jul 12, 2019. "I am not a fan of Bitcoin..." The price fell 10.9% within a week. This tweet is often cited as proof that Trump moves crypto, but 2019 was also thick with regulatory uncertainty that had nothing to do with any single post.

The outlier. Mar 2, 2025. A U.S. Crypto Reserve announcement, about as bullish as presidential crypto news gets. Bitcoin dropped 14.5% over the following week anyway. Good news posts do not guarantee gains; markets often price the policy before the post ever lands.

In office vs. out of office. Average one-week returns after Bitcoin posts, split by presidency era. Holding the office does not automatically mean stronger moves. Context and market regime matter more than the title under the username.

Source: Power BI report. Event study on 73,380 cleaned posts (2009 to 2025).

Market 02

Oil

Crude oil is one of the most geopolitically sensitive commodities in the world. War, OPEC supply decisions, inventory reports, and global demand shifts routinely move prices far more than any social media post ever could.

Trump touches Iran and energy 705 times in our tagged dataset, yet the average one-week return after those posts is slightly negative. In the aggregate, he is an insignificant factor for oil. We study the patterns anyway, because timing around acute crises, like the June 2025 Iran standoff, still tells an interesting story.

Price context, 2024 to 2025. Oil traded between roughly $55 and $90 per barrel. The spikes in early 2025 lined up with Middle East tension, not with posts alone.

June 2025, the Iran cluster. Each bar is one Trump post and the oil return one week later. The Jun 12 diplomacy post preceded a +10.1% weekly move, but that same month was packed with military headlines and supply shocks. The coloring highlights magnitude, not causation.

Oil moves for many reasons. Treat post-event returns as timing snapshots, not trade signals.

Market 03

S&P 500

The S&P 500 tracks roughly 500 large U.S. companies and is the broadest single measure of American equity health. Trump posts about tariffs, China, and the economy more than any other topic: 1,772 one-day events in this study.

On average the index drifts slightly higher after his posts, +0.3% at one week and +1.5% at one month. That sounds bullish until you remember that most of 2009 to 2025 was a secular bull market. Trump is one voice among thousands of daily inputs pushing this index around.

Daily close, 2024 to 2025. The index ranged from about 5,000 to nearly 6,900 points, including a violent April 2025 drawdown and recovery tied to tariff headlines and broader macro fear.

Apr 8, 2025. Auto tariffs. A post about steering jobs back to the U.S. preceded an +8.3% one-week rally. Did the post cause the move, or did it simply coincide with a relief rally after weeks of tariff panic? The chart shows price before and after the event date so you can judge for yourself.

Market 04

Nasdaq

The Nasdaq Composite is tech-heavy: Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, and hundreds of growth names. Trump's posts about Big Tech regulation and his election-eve rhetoric map cleanly onto this index.

Across 74 Nasdaq-tagged events, the one-month win rate is 79%, the highest in the study, with an average one-month return of +3.0%. Before reading too much into that, remember that tech outperformed nearly everything for fifteen years. Trump is not the primary driver here either.

Index context. Nasdaq performance alongside the headline statistics from the event study: average returns, win rates, and event counts.

Nov 2, 2020. Election eve. A post attacking Biden and Big Tech preceded a +6.9% one-week Nasdaq gain. It also landed hours before a presidential election, one of the noisiest weeks in market history. Association, not attribution.

Software

When to invest?

This is the Python-built interactive layer, running on the same event-study engine as the data pipeline. Pick a curated Trump post, choose an amount, and see what a hypothetical investment placed on the day of the post would have returned one week or one month later.

What it tells you: historical outcomes on specific dates. What it does not tell you: that the post caused the return, or that the next post will behave the same way.

Documentation

About this project

A full-stack data analysis portfolio piece: raw social media data through Python, SQL modeling, Power BI visualization, and this site, deployed on Vercel.

Data sources

  • Trump posts: 90,343 raw records cleaned down to 73,380 posts (2009 to 2025). Deleted posts, pure reposts, and empty text removed.
  • Market prices: Yahoo Finance daily OHLC for the S&P 500, Nasdaq, Dow, VIX, Bitcoin, WTI crude oil, and gold.
  • Event definitions: keyword topic tagging (Bitcoin, oil and Iran, S&P, Nasdaq, tariffs, and more) plus a posting-day event study.
  • Forward returns: +1 day, +1 week (5 trading days), and +1 month (21 trading days) from each event date.
  • Featured stories: 12 hand-curated events with original post text, used in the calculator and the narrative tabs.

Technology stack

Python

Data cleaning, VADER sentiment, yfinance market pulls, event study, DuckDB star schema, and the CSV catalog feeding Power BI

Power BI

Interactive report: price context, event timelines, in-office comparisons, and conditional formatting on returns

SQL

Star schema (Fact_Posts, Fact_Market, Dim_Date) with window functions and correlation queries

Cursor + Claude

AI-assisted development environment used for pipeline design, DAX debugging, and building this website

This site

Hand-written HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No framework. Deployed on Vercel with charts exported from the Power BI report.

GitHub

Fully reproducible repository: scripts, data catalog, Power BI files, and website source

Methodology

  • Sentiment scored before any market data joins, to avoid look-ahead bias
  • Aggregate daily sentiment shows almost no correlation with returns. The signal lives in specific events.
  • All averages include every tagged event. No cherry-picked winners.
  • Associations reported honestly. External factors (war, the Fed, earnings) are always present.
